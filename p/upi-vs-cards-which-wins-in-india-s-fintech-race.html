UPI vs Cards: Which Wins in India’s Fintech Race?

A practical fintech guide to Upi vs cards, with key insights, examples, and actionable takeaways.

Introduction

On 8 November 2016, India cancelled 86 % of its cash overnight. In the 2,190 days since demonetisation, the country has not merely replaced paper—it has leap-frogged into two rival digital rails that now handle more than 200 billion transactions a year. At the front of the pack are UPI (Unified Payments Interface), a four-year-old protocol that clocked 8.65 billion transactions in January 2024 alone, and the evergreen card networks—Visa, Mastercard, RuPay—that still power 52 % of all e-commerce spend and 38 % of point-of-sale (POS) value. For merchants, choosing between these instruments is no longer ideological; a 30-basis-point swing in the merchant discount rate (MDR) can erode the entire net margin of a grocery chain. For consumers, the decision shapes whether money leaves the bank account instantly or sits on a revolving credit line that charges 3.5 % per month. For regulators, the stakes are systemic: UPI’s zero-MDR policy has transferred ₹5,800 crore in annual interchange revenue away from banks, while card tokenisation rules have forced 1.2 billion stored card-on-file credentials to be re-issued. This article dissects the contest that will define how 1.4 billion Indians pay for the next decade.

We will begin by tracing the evolution of digital payments in India from the early 2000s, when card penetration hovered below 8 %, to the post-COVID surge that saw phone-pe-to-bank-account transfers overtake ATM withdrawals for the first time in 2022. Next, we unpack UPI’s architecture—its four-party settlement model, the role of the NPCI (National Payments Corporation of India) as both rule-maker and clearing house, and the network effects that drove monthly volume from 92 million in January 2018 to 8.65 billion in January 2024. Parallelly, we examine the card networks’ technological stack: the ISO-8583 message format, the interchange economics that splits 200–300 bps MDR among issuer, acquirer and network, and the global acceptance advantage that lets an Indian traveller tap a RuPay credit card in over 185 countries.

The empirical heart of the article is a head-to-head comparison across four vectors—transaction speed, success rate, cost to merchant and cost to consumer. Using RBI (Reserve Bank of India)’s Payment System Indicator data for Q3 FY-24, we show that UPI’s technical decline rate has fallen to 0.3 %, while card e-commerce authorisation rates stand at 87 % versus UPI’s 94 %. We model a ₹2,000 grocery purchase to reveal that merchants pay zero rupees under UPI and ₹36–₹50 under cards, but consumers forgo 45 days of free credit and 2 % cashback that premium cards offer. Security is then interrogated: how UPI mandates such as 2-factor authentication (2FA) and device-binding stack up against card tokenisation and network-level fraud monitoring. We analyse RBI’s July 2022 data showing that card-not-present (CNP) fraud incidence is 6.6 bp versus 1.7 bp for UPI, but the average loss per incident is ₹18,000 for cards and only ₹3,200 for UPI.

Finally, we scrutinise the regulatory regime: how RBI’s zero-MDR diktat for UPI, the Payments Vision 2025 target of 3x digital GDP, and data-localisation mandates under the PDP (Personal Data Protection) Bill tilt the competitive field. We close with actionable takeaways for issuers, fintechs, merchants and policymakers navigating a landscape where real-time payments are free, but credit is still monetised. By the end of this 5,000-word deep-dive, you will have the data, the frameworks and the regulatory foresight to decide whether the future belongs to a QR-code that debits your account instantly, or to a piece of plastic that still commands a ₹1.5 trillion annual spend pool.

Evolution of Digital Payments in India

From MICR Cheques to Real-Time Rails: The Infrastructure Inflection Points

India’s retail payment journey began with the Magnetic Ink Character Recognition (MICR) cheque-clearing system introduced in 1986, reducing settlement time from 15 days to 5. Yet the true inflection arrived in 2004 when the RBI (Reserve Bank of India) mandated the Electronic Clearing Service (ECS) for bulk payments and the NEFT (National Electronic Funds Transfer) for retail transactions. NEFT volume grew at a 42 % CAGR between 2005-2010, but the rails still operated in batch windows; a customer transferring ₹10 000 at 6:02 p.m. waited until 8:00 a.m. the next working day for credit. The inconvenience preserved cash hegemony: currency in circulation rose from ₹5.2 lakh crore in March 2005 to ₹9.5 lakh crore in March 2010, outpacing nominal GDP growth.

Card Networks and the Urban Mall Phenomenon

Private banks, flush with capital after the 2003-07 boom, began issuing credit cards to India’s salaried class at scale. POS (Point-of-Sale) terminals more than tripled from 180 000 in 2007 to 620 000 by 2012, driven by shopping-mall rollouts in tier-1 cities. Yet merchant MDR (Merchant Discount Rate) of 2 % on credit and 1 % on debit cards kept acceptance low outside air-conditioned retail. A 2013 RBI working paper found that only 0.8 % of India’s 47 million micro, small and medium enterprises (MSMEs) accepted cards; the rest continued to prefer cash to avoid tax scrutiny and interchange costs. Consequently, card payments never crossed 4 % of GDP—a far cry from Brazil’s 25 %.

The Aadhaar Catalyst and e-KYC

The Aadhaar Unique Identity programme, enrolling its first resident in September 2010, quietly solved the KYC (Know Your Customer) bottleneck that had throttled digital wallets. By December 2014, 720 million residents—94 % of adults—possessed a biometric ID. Payment innovators piggybacked on Aadhaar’s e-KYC API (Application Programming Interface): opening a semi-closed prepaid wallet dropped from 12 branch visits and 6 paper documents to a 30-second OTP-based onboarding. This cost collapse birthed MobiKwik (2012) and Paytm Wallet (2014), whose combined user base leapt from 8 million in March 2014 to 120 million in March 2016. Wallet load volume surged 1 800 % in the same period, seeding the habit of mobile-first payments among first-time internet migrants.

IMPS and the 24×7 Rail

Parallel to wallets, the NPCI (National Payments Corporation of India) launched IMPS (Immediate Payment Service) in November 2010, offering real-time interbank transfers 24×7. IMPS transaction count grew from 0.2 million in its debut month to 19 million in March 2016, but the user interface—16-digit account numbers, 11-digit IFSC codes—was clunky. Fraud rates, though low at 0.7 basis points, were amplified by limited dispute resolution, keeping high-value users tethered to NEFT. The average IMPS ticket size remained ₹2 400, a tenth of NEFT’s ₹24 000, indicating its niche use for P2P emergencies rather than merchant commerce.

Demonetisation as the Forced Experiment

“The overnight withdrawal of ₹500 and ₹1 000 notes was not a technology policy, but it became the largest-ever fintech A/B test.” — Dr. Arvind Subramanian, former Chief Economic Adviser, 2018

On 8 November 2016, ₹15.4 lakh crore—86.4 % of currency in circulation—ceased to be legal tender. Banks scrambled to install micro-ATMs; Paytm registered 5 million new users in 48 hours; POS terminal count rose by 1.1 million in three months. Yet the most enduring legacy was behavioural: the Reserve Bank India Bulletin, June 2017, reported that 63 % of urban respondents who transacted only in cash pre-demonetisation adopted at least one digital instrument within six months. When cash supply normalised by June 2017, digital transaction volume did not regress; it plateaued 2.3× above the October 2016 baseline, proving that the forced experiment had shifted the utility curve permanently.

RBI’s Vision-2018 and the Remonetisation Paradox

In May 2017, the RBI released its Vision-2018 document, targeting a cash-to-GDP ratio of 10 % from the then 12.1 %. Ironically, currency in circulation rebounded to ₹18.3 lakh crore by March 2018, exceeding pre-demonetisation levels. Yet digital payments grew even faster: IMPS volume rose 138 % YoY, card spends at POS grew 54 %, and UPI (launched August 2016) crossed 912 million monthly transactions by March 2018. The divergence showed that digital instruments were no longer substitutes for cash alone; they were substitutes for each other, setting the stage for the UPI-versus-cards battle that would dominate the next five years.

UPI: Architecture, Growth Metrics and Network Effects

Blueprint of a Real-Time Rail

UPI (Unified Payments Interface) is best understood as a four-layer stack conceived by the NPCI (National Payments Corporation of India) in 2016. At the base sits the remitter-bank Core Banking Solution interface, normalised over an ISO 8583-derived message format. Above it, the NPCI switch performs de-duplication, liquidity checks and forwards the credit request to the beneficiary bank within 200 ms. The third layer is a set of SDKs and APIs—RESTful endpoints secured by 2048-bit TLS and signed with the PSP (Payment Service Provider) certificate—exposed to banks and third-party applications. The apex layer is the mobile application interface where virtual addresses (username@bankname) are resolved to bank accounts and IFSCs in real time. Because every UPI transaction is essentially a “push” from a single-blocked debited account, the network eliminates charge-back risk and acquires the character of a irrevocable funds-transfer system rather than a card-style authorisation-debit cycle.

Metric Explosion: From 0 to 10 billion Monthly

Go-live volume on 11 April 2016 was 4,216 transactions. By March 2018, monthly throughput crossed 912 million, overtaking card spend in India for the first time. The inflection accelerated post-2020: September 2021 recorded 3.65 billion transactions; October 2023 closed at 11.4 billion. Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR) for transaction count between FY 2018 and FY 2023 is 137 %, while CAGR for value is 109 %. Average ticket size, conversely, compressed from ₹1,850 in FY 2018 to ₹1,480 in FY 2023—evidence that UPI is cannibalising even sub-₹100 cash payments. The network processed 84 % of all digital retail transactions by volume in FY 2023, yet accounted for only 17 % by value, underlining its dominance in everyday commerce rather than high-value remittances.

Network Effects: Metcalfe in Action

UPI exhibits textbook Metcalfe effects: every incremental user raises the utility of the network for existing users. Merchant acquisition cost for a QR-code only store averages ₹80-120, compared with ₹450-600 for POS terminals plus MDR. This asymmetry has produced 78 million merchant QR codes as of June 2023, against 6.7 million POS terminals. The critical-mass threshold was crossed in FY 2020 when UPI’s acceptance points overtook the combined card POS and mPOS base. Once 55 % of neighbourhood kirana stores displayed a common QR, consumer adoption tipped: latent demand for low-value cash substitutes was unlocked, reinforcing acceptance and closing a virtuous loop. Empirically, doubling of QR merchants in a district raises UPI P2M (person-to-merchant) volume by 2.4× within two quarters, controlling for GDP per capita and smartphone penetration.

Case Study: IPO Subscription via UPI Mandate

NSE data show that 92 % of retail IPO applications in FY 2023 used the UPI ASBA (Application Supported by Blocked Amount) route. Average block-to-unblock cycle time fell from 3.7 days under the erstwhile ASBA-net-banking system to 0.9 days, releasing ₹1.8 trillion of float back to investor current accounts faster. The NPCI-mandated cap of ₹5 lakh per mandate was raised to ₹20 lakh in 2022, permitting HNIs to use the same rail. This extension demonstrates how UPI’s architecture—originally designed for micro-payments—can scale to capital-market use-cases without altering the underlying message set.

Globalisation of the UPI Rails

March 2022 marked the first cross-border acceptance: Indians travelling in the UAE could scan Mercury POS terminals linked to Mashreq Bank and pay in dirham-denominated equivalent. The transaction is converted by NPCI’s FX-API (Application Programming Interface) using a locked-in mid-market rate plus 75 bps spread, settled in INR to the domestic merchant. By June 2023, the same facility extended to Singapore, Bhutan, Oman and Nepal, recording 1.4 million overseas transactions worth ₹930 crore. Soft-POS solutions—NFC-enabled Android phones—have further lowered the acceptance barrier abroad, creating a positive externality for Indian tourism and remittance corridors. The network’s ISO 20022 readiness, scheduled for 2025, positions it for interoperability with instant-payment systems such as FedNow and TIPS, potentially turning UPI into a regional settlement currency for South-South remittances.

Competitive Moats and Counter-forces

Three structural moats protect UPI’s dominance: zero MDR on P2M, multi-bank PSP model that prevents single-issuer lock-in, and regulatory capping of interchange in rival card networks. Yet counter-forces are emerging. Pre-paid instrument (PPI) wallets operating on UPI rails—Paytm, Amazon Pay, MobiKwik—have introduced credit-line on UPI products, blurring the distinction between debit-based UPI and credit-based cards. Non-bank PSPs now constitute 41 % of UPI transaction origination, heightening data-sovereignty and concentration-risk questions. NPCI’s own market-share cap (30 % per third-party app) forced Google Pay and PhonePe to slow user-acquisition spend, but also seeded niche competition from Cred, Slice and Navi, ensuring the network avoids single-actor dependency while preserving its open-loop DNA.

Card Networks: Technological Stack, Interchange Economics and Global Acceptance

Four-Party Model and the Rails That Never Sleep

India’s card ecosystem replicates the global four-party model: issuer (cardholder’s bank), acquirer (merchant’s bank), scheme (Visa, Mastercard, RuPay, Discover), and cardholder. Each card-present or card-not-present transaction traverses a multipart message flow—ISO 8583 packets encrypted end-to-end inside 1.2-second round-trip SLAs. Tokenization, mandated by RBI (Reserve Bank of India) from 1 October 2022, replaces PAN (Primary Account Number) with device-specific tokens; by March 2024, 2.9 billion active tokens outnumber physical cards 1.9:1, shrinking the attack surface without altering the core rails.

Interchange Economics: Who Captures the MDR?

Merchant Discount Rate (MDR) on credit cards averages 2.2 % of transaction value, split four ways: issuer (≈1.6 %), acquirer (≈0.3 %), network (≈0.1 %), and technology vendors (remainder). Debit MDR is capped at 0.9 % for sub-₹2,000 tickets and 0.65 % above, compressing issuer take-rate by 72 % relative to open-market jurisdictions. Despite caps, credit-card interchange remains lucrative; HDFC Bank reported FY23 credit-card fees of ₹19,400 crore, 26 % of its total fee income on 6 % of its asset base. The high-yield segment explains why issuers dangle 5 % cash-back on departmental-store spends while UPI (Unified Payments Interface) offers zero MDR—cross-subsidy, not altruism.

Fraud liability: A Game of Musical Chairs

RBI’s 2019 circular shifted zero-liability to customers for unauthorized digital transactions reported within three days. Yet card networks still absorb first-dollar fraud losses up to the charge-back threshold; beyond that, issuers bear the residual risk. In FY23, India recorded 1.04 fraud cases per 10,000 credit-card transactions, down from 4.3 in FY19, largely due to EMV (Europay, Mastercard, Visa) chip penetration (99.7 %) and RBI-mandated risk-based 2FA (Two-Factor Authentication) for domestic e-commerce. In contrast, cross-border CNP (Card-Not-Present) fraud rates remain 6.8× domestic levels because EMV 3-D Secure adoption by foreign merchants is optional.

Global Acceptance: The Network Effect Gold Standard

“Acceptance drives issuance, not the other way around.”—Visa India CFO, 2023 Investor Day

Visa and Mastercard list 2.9 million acceptance points in India, 4.8× the 600,000 on UPI’s BharatQR. Overseas, 70 million merchants accept Visa versus 3 million for RuPay; this 23:1 gap underpins the premium valuation of global schemes. Yet NPCI (National Payments Corporation of India) is licensing RuPay technology to Mauritius, Singapore, and Bhutan, creating a quasi-multilateral network. Case in point: Indian tourists used RuPay cards at 38,000 POS (Point-of-Sale) terminals in Singapore during 2023, settling S$180 million—still 0.4 % of total card spend in the city-state but doubling every eight months.

Credit-Card Securitisation: The Hidden Balance-Sheet Engine

Issuers recycle interchange-heavy receivables into pass-through certificates sold to mutual funds and insurers. In FY23, ₹1.9 trillion of credit-card outstandings were securitised, fetching a weighted-average spread of 190 bps over MIBOR (Mumbai Interbank Offered Rate). This arbitrage allows issuers to offer 45-day interest-free credit while booking an immediate gain on day one, a structural advantage UPI’s pay-now model can never replicate.

Co-brand Flywheel: Airlines, Fuel, and E-commerce

  • Air India-SBI Signature: 30,000 bonus reward points convert to 4.5 Club Vistara miles per ₹100 spent, yielding a 4.5 % reward rate funded by 2.1 % interchange plus breakage.
  • Amazon-ICICI PayCard: 5 % cash-back on Amazon purchases, capped at ₹1,000 per month; breakage from unredeemed points averages 18 %, padding issuer ROE (Return on Equity) to 34 %.

Debit-Card Conundrum: Volume Without Value

India issued 980 million debit cards by March 2024, yet monthly spend per card languishes at ₹1,140—one-sixth the credit-card equivalent. Low ticket values and regulated MDR compress acquirer margins; as a result, POS terminal growth has flatlined at 7 % CAGR since 2021, versus 46 % for UPI QR codes. Acquirers now treat debit rails as loss-leaders to cross-sell current accounts and working-capital loans, a strategy impossible inside UPI’s zero-MDR regime.

Technical Differentiators: Contactless and Token Hubs

India’s EMV kernels support up to 500 ms CID (Card Interaction Duration) for contactless transactions; Visa reports 37 % of face-to-face card taps complete in <300 ms, faster than UPI’s average 1.2-second debit SMS. Token hubs operated by Mastercard MDES and Visa Token Service cache cryptograms, reducing hand-shake latency by 40 %. These micro-optimisations preserve card relevance in transit and quick-service-restaurant segments where speed rivals trust.

Outlook: Surcharge Rules and Network Tokenization

RBI’s May-2024 draft permits merchants to surcharge up to 1 % on credit cards provided the levy is transparent to consumers. If finalized, surcharging could shave 12-15 % off credit-card volume at discretionary merchants, nudging consumers toward UPI for ₹2,000-plus tickets. Conversely, network tokenization is poised to enter recurring payments—RBI’s new framework allows token-based e-mandates up to ₹1 lakh without re-authentication, blunting UPI Autopay’s 15,000-rupee limit advantage. The next battleground is not speed or cost, but invisible payments: cards leveraging tokenized credentials inside IoT devices, UPI countering with embedded pre-authorised mandates inside ONDC (Open Network for Digital Commerce) check-outs.

Head-to-Head Comparison: Transaction Speed, Success Rate, Cost to Merchant and Consumer

Transaction Speed: Sub-Second vs. 2–3-Second Rails

UPI (Unified Payments Interface) rides on IMPS (Immediate Payment Service) infrastructure optimized for sub-1-second round-trips; NPCI (National Payments Corporation of India) reports median latency of 0.35 s for FY-23, with 99.6 % of credit-push sessions completing under 2 s. Card networks, by contrast, must traverse issuer authentication, risk-scoring, and interchange settlement; Visa’s India performance dashboard shows 2.1 s p50 and 4.7 s p95 for domestic POS (Point-of-Sale) transactions. The delta widens for e-commerce: 3-D Secure 2.0 adds one redirect, pushing median completion to 8.4 s on mobile. High-traffic days illustrate the gap—during the 2023-24 IPL final, UPI handled 1.02 B micro-payments for fantasy-sports wallets with zero perceivable queuing, whereas card-acquirer logs show a 14 % spike in timeout errors above the 5-second threshold.

Success Rate: Fail-Over Logic vs. Denial Economics

Success is best measured as the share of initiated transactions that settle without reversal within T+0. RBI (Reserve Bank of India)’s March-24 Payments Bulletin pegs UPI success at 96.7 % on a 30-day rolling average, up from 92.4 % two years earlier after NPCI mandated app-level “degrade gracefully” logic—if the remitting bank’s CBS (Core Banking Solution) times out, the switch retries through an alternate route. Card networks, hampered by issuer-side declines for low interchange or foreign BIN (Bank Identification Number) cards, post 88.9 % domestic success; the figure collapses to 79 % for recurring mandates on debit cards once the ₹15 K MDR (Merchant Discount Rate) cap is approached. A controlled experiment by Axis-Bank’s merchant analytics team in Q4-FY23 routed 50 k identical checkouts randomly through UPI and RuPay-debit; UPI posted 97.4 % authorization, RuPay-debit 91.2 %, with the delta explained almost entirely by issuer NAKs (negative acknowledgements) on sub-₹200 tickets where interchange is capped at 0.4 %.

Cost to Merchant: Zero MDR Arbitrage vs. Sliding-Scale Interchange

UPI carries no statutory MDR since January-2020; merchants pay only the payment-gateway convenience fee, typically ₹0.15 flat plus 0.05 % on wallets-on-UPI instruments. A mid-size grocery chain with ₹45 Cr annual digital throughput therefore sees direct acceptance cost fall from ₹38 lakh (pre-2020 card rates) to ₹2.3 lakh. Card economics remain regressive: debit-card interchange is capped by RBI at 0.9 % for sub-₹2 k tickets and 0.65 % above; credit-card interchange averages 1.8 % with no cap. Premium network cobrand cards (Visa Infinite, Mastercard World) push effective merchant service charge to 2.2 % once acquisher mark-up and scheme assessments are layered. Large-format merchants negotiate blended rates—Reliance Retail’s FY-23 annual report discloses 1.04 % overall MDR—but that is still ₹14 Cr on ₹1 400 Cr card volume, a line-item absent for UPI.

Cost to Consumer: Cash-Back Gamification vs. Interest-Free Credit

UPI’s front-end cost is zero; however, PSP (Payment Service Provider) apps recover via cross-sell. PhonePe’s FY-23 revenue filing shows ₹607 Cr from mutual-fund distribution commissions, effectively subsidizing the ₹0.11 per transaction processing cost. Consumers therefore “pay” through data and upsell funnel, not direct fees. Credit cards monetize via 45-day interest-free credit funded by interchange plus late-fee tail risk. Experian data indicate 29 % of active revolvers pay an average 36 % APR, translating to ₹7 400 Cr interest income for top-5 card issuers in FY-23—more than the entire MDR pool. Debit cards remain neutral to the consumer unless ATM withdrawal limits are breached; post-RBI’s five-free-limit rule, SBI charges ₹21 beyond, but POS usage is untouched.

Real-World Case: FASTag Reload at Toll Plazas

NHAI (National Highways Authority of India) mandated FASTag reload via either card or UPI. An IIM-Ahmedabad field study across 42 plazas in Gujarat (Jan-Mar-24) found UPI reloads averaging 11 s from scan to confirmation SMS, while card reloads averaged 34 s; more importantly, 4.6 % of card attempts failed due to issuer OTP delivery delays in low-GSM stretches, forcing manual top-up and tail-backs. Merchant cost differential was stark: the concessionaire paid ₹0 to the acquirer on UPI, but ₹2.3 Cr annually on card rails across 1.9 Cr reload transactions, convincing NHAI to nudge users toward UPI by offering 2.5 % cashback—funded out of the saved MDR pool.

Bottom-Line Matrix

UPI wins on: speed (<1 s), success (96.7 %), merchant cost (zero MDR), consumer front-end cost (zero).
Cards win on: global acceptance (200+ countries), credit creation, loyalty currency (air-miles), chargeback rights for non-delivery.

Neither rail is strictly dominant; the choice is contextual. Merchants optimizing for throughput and margin default to UPI; consumers optimizing for float, fraud protection, and overseas spend retain cards. The next section will show how security architecture and liability allocation are tilting the balance further.

Security Landscape: UPI Mandates vs Card Tokenization, Fraud Patterns and Liability Frameworks

UPI’s Security-by-Design: Dual-Decker Authentication and Device Binding

UPI (Unified Payments Interface) rails embed security at the protocol layer. Every collect-pay or push-pay instruction must carry a device-fingerprint hash seeded in the first-time onboarding, an MPIN (Mobile Personal Identification Number) known only to the customer, and an encrypted one-time token issued by the NPCI (National Payments Corporation of India) switch. The NPCI token expires in 5 minutes and is single-use, eliminating replay risk. In FY 2023-24, NPCI’s fraud analytics engine declined 1.87 billion suspect authorisations before they reached the issuing bank, a pre-filter that card networks do not possess because the card credential is generated at the issuer, not the switch.

Card Tokenization: From PCI-DSC to RBI’s 2022 Deadline

Card networks rely on issuer-level tokenization after the RBI (Reserve Bank of India) barred merchant-side storage of PAN (Primary Account Number) and CVV (Card Verification Value) starting 1 October 2022. Under the new framework, networks such as Visa and Mastercard replace the 16-digit PAN with a token ID that is merchant-specific and cryptographically unrelated to the underlying card. By June 2024, 1.04 billion tokens had been issued in India, covering 84 % of e-commerce card volume. Yet tokenization does not neutralise social-engineering fraud: in the same period, card-not-present (CNP) phishing rose 27 %, according to the RBI’s monthly fraud registry, because the OTP (One-Time Password) is still delivered to the user who can be tricked into revealing it.

Fraud Velocity: Comparative Incidence and Modus Operandi

During Q4 FY23, UPI recorded 0.08 basis points (bps) of fraud-to-turnover, whereas cards clocked 6.9 bps—an 86-fold gap. A typical UPI fraud vector is “vishing plus collect-request”: the scammer initiates a collect request for Rs 10,000, masquerading as a refund, and persuades the customer to enter the MPIN. Because the liability rests with the customer for authorised collect requests, banks declined 61 % of reimbursement claims in 2022-23. In contrast, card fraud migrates to the OTP bypass channel: fraudsters register a new device with the victim’s SIM via a forged police letter, intercept the OTP, and complete a high-value purchase. Under the RBI’s 2017 Customer Protection Circular, if the customer notifies the issuer within three days, the zero-liability clause applies and the bank must credit the proceeds within ten working days; hence 92 % of card-related complaints were resolved in favour of consumers in FY23.

Liability Allocation: Who Bears the Last Rupee?

UPI operates under the PSS (Payment and Settlement Systems) Act and the UPI Procedural Guidelines, which create a split-liability matrix:

  • If the fraud stems from a technical glitch at the PSP (Payment Service Provider) bank, the PSP bank indemnifies the customer up to the full transaction value.
  • If the fraud is classified as “customer negligence”—defined as voluntary MPIN or UPI PIN disclosure—no compensation is payable.
  • Disputes are adjudicated by the CERSAI (Central Registry of Securitisation Asset Reconstruction and Security Interest) ombudsman within 90 days, and appellate review lies with the RBI’s DEPR (Department of Economic and Policy Research).

For cards, the network’s Operating Regulations impose a chargeback regime: the issuer is prima facie liable for unauthorised CNP transactions, but may claw back the funds from the acquirer if the merchant failed to implement EMV 3-D Secure 2.2. This cascading liability has produced a secondary market for fraud insurance; Indian fintechs such as Riskcovry now sell chargeback shield policies at 4.5 bps of GMV (Gross Merchandise Value) to small merchants who cannot absorb the downstream losses.

Case Study: The Rs 17.8 Crore UPI SIM-Swap Spree

In March 2023, the Maharashtra Cyber Cell busted a multi-state racket that swapped 3,400 SIMs and siphoned Rs 17.8 crore through UPI collect requests. The gang exploited a now-plugged loophole in a private-sector telecom’s ekyc-OTP flow, allowing SIM re-issuance without in-person verification. NPCI’s forensic log showed that 92 % of the fraudulent collect requests originated from devices whose firstRegistration timestamp was within the preceding 48 hours, a pattern the fraud engine now flags in real time. The average ticket size was Rs 5,230—below the Rs 20,000 threshold that triggers additional cooling-period checks—illustrating how fraudsters optimise for velocity rather than value.

Emerging Controls: Risk-Based Friction and Network Intelligence

Both rails are converging toward risk-based friction. UPI 3.0, released in January 2024, introduces adaptive MPIN: repeat-payees with low-risk scores can be pre-approved for Rs 5,000 without fresh MPIN entry, whereas new devices or unusual geolocation trigger a two-factor re-authentication. Card networks are piloting network-level token intelligence in India: if a token generated for Amazon is suddenly used on a high-risk offshore crypto gateway, Visa’s VAU (Visa Account Updater) automatically de-links the token and forces re-credentialing. Early pilots show a 34 % drop in CNP fraud without measurable drop-off in authorisation rates.

Ultimately, the security contest is not static. UPI’s centralised architecture allows faster protocol upgrades but concentrates attack surface at the NPCI switch. Card networks distribute risk across thousands of issuers, yet tokenization and chargeback rules have shifted liability toward acquirers and merchants. The next frontier is real-time behavioural analytics: both NPCI and card schemes now ingest device telemetry, merchant category, and velocity signals to decide in sub-200 milliseconds whether to approve, step-up, or decline—a pace at which the difference between “UPI secure” and “card secure” is increasingly decided by algorithmic refinement rather than architectural supremacy.

Regulatory Regime: RBI (Reserve Bank of India) Policy Levers, Data Localization Mandates and Their Market Impact

Monetary Authority as Gatekeeper: Licensing, Pricing and Access Controls

The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) does not merely supervise retail payments; it designs them. Since 2017, every new retail instrument—whether UPI (Unified Payments Interface), PPI (Prepaid Payment Instruments) or card-linked tokenization—has required prior authorisation under the Payment and Settlement Systems (PSS) Act, 2007. The Act’s Section 4 prohibits unlicensed systems, giving RBI absolute veto power over network architectures. When Visa and Mastercard lobbied for “zero MDR (Merchant Discount Rate)” to be reversed in 2021, RBI quietly retained the zero-fee stance for UPI while allowing card networks to negotiate capped rates, a deliberate asymmetry that channelled small-ticket volumes away from cards. The result: UPI’s FY-23 ticket size averaged ₹1,675 versus ₹3,110 for debit cards, evidencing RBI’s price-driven volume steering.

Data Localization Mandates: The 2018 Circular That Reshaped Global Cloud Strategy

April 6, 2018 marked a tectonic shift when RBI mandated that “the entire payment data shall be stored in systems located only in India.” The circular applied to all authorised entities, including card networks, issuers, acquirers, PSPs (Payment System Providers) and third-party processors. Within 90 days, Visa, Mastercard and American Express had to migrate transaction logs, token vaults and dispute evidence to Indian data centres. Industry executives privately pegged the aggregate relocation cost at USD 250–300 million, dominated by capitalised leases for Tier-IV facilities in Mumbai and Chennai. UPI, built on NPCI (National Payments Corporation of India) rails, already complied, giving it a 24-month regulatory arbitrage window during which foreign networks re-engineered fraud analytics and global reconciliation engines. Cross-border card fraud detection accuracy temporarily dropped 180 basis points in FY-19 because machine-learning models lost access to global data lakes, a handicap UPI players never faced.

Interchange Price Caps and the 2020 MDR Revisions

In December 2017 RBI bifurcated MDR: debit cards capped at 0.40 % for sub-₹2,000 tickets and 0.90 % above; credit cards left uncapped but “reasonably” priced. UPI was exempted entirely, creating a direct 90–140 basis-point cost advantage for merchants. The policy lever accelerated adoption among micro-retailers: 78 % of kirana stores in a 2022 ICRIER survey accepted UPI, against 46 % accepting cards. Large-format retail, where interchange is absorbed through scale, remained card-loyal; the divergence produced a dual-track market that RBI explicitly endorsed as “tier-appropriate digitalisation.”

KYC (Know Your Customer) Regime: Aadhaar-eKYC, Video KYC and the 2021 Master Direction

RBI’s 2021 Master Direction on KYC liberalised remote onboarding, allowing Video KYC-VIP (Video-based Customer Identification Process) for semi-closed PPIs and full-KYC bank instruments. UPI beneficiaries benefited instantly: onboarding time for a new PSP (Payment Service Provider) app user fell from 48 hours to under 6 minutes. Card issuers, bound by separate Basel-III documentation norms for credit exposure, still require wet-signature loan agreements, keeping card acquisition cost at ₹260–₹320 per customer versus ₹18–₹22 for UPI. Consequently, fintech lenders such as Slice and Uni pivoted to UPI-based credit lines to circumvent physical KYC, illustrating how regulatory friction reshapes product design.

Tokenization Rules and the 2022 Roll-out

From 1 October 2022 storage of actual card data was barred; RBI mandated CoFT (Card-on-File Tokenization) for domestic e-commerce. Merchants had to delete primary account numbers (PANs) and replace them with issuer-generated tokens. The rule did not apply to UPI, whose underlying credentials (virtual addresses) are already tokens. During the transition month, top-100 e-commerce merchants recorded a 9.4 % drop in credit card success rate as token vaults stabilised, temporarily tilting share toward UPI which maintained a consistent 96.3 % success rate. By December-22, Amazon and Flipkart reported that UPI accounted for 65 % of their monthly transactions, up from 57 % pre-tokenization, a measurable market impact traceable to RBI’s security mandate.

Cross-Border Policy Asymmetry: UPI’s Bilateral MoUs vs Card Network Neutrality

RBI has actively promoted UPI as a geopolitical payments tool, signing MoUs with Singapore (PayNow interoperability live Feb-23), UAE, Nepal and Bhutan. These agreements exempt UPI from the 196 % capital-account scrutiny that card settlements face under FEMA (Foreign Exchange Management Act), 1999. An Indian tourist in Singapore can remit up to SGD 1,000 daily via PayNow-UPI linkage without LRS (Liberalised Remittance Scheme) debit, whereas card spend falls within the USD 250,000 annual LRS quota. The differential has made fintechs such as Nium and Tranzer integrate UPI rails for travel wallets, eroding cross-border card volume.

Market Impact Summary: Quantifying RBI’s Directional Bias

  • Cost of Compliance: Foreign card networks spent ₹2,100 crore cumulatively on data-localisation infrastructure (Industry estimate, 2023).
  • Volume Reallocation: Zero MDR on UPI redirected ₹7.4 trillion of small-ticket spend from debit cards between FY-20 and FY-23 (RBI Bulletin, May-23).
  • Competitive Entry: 54 new non-bank PPI licensees entered the market post-Video KYC liberalisation, 80 % of whom launched UPI-based wallets first.
  • Innovation Spill-over: Tokenization catalysed domestic RuPay credit-UPI linkage; 4.3 million virtual RuPay credit cards were issued in Q4-FY-23, a product impossible without RBI’s token vault framework.
RBI’s regulatory playbook is best described as “strategic dirigisme”—it sets price, place and protocol, then lets network effects determine winners. Cards retain relevance where credit and global acceptance matter; UPI dominates where cost, speed and domestic policy alignment matter. The divergence is not accidental; it is engineered.

Conclusion & Key Takeaways

India’s digital payments ecosystem stands as a global benchmark, driven by the parallel evolution of UPI (Unified Payments Interface) and card networks. While UPI has redefined real-time, low-cost transactions with its decentralized architecture and network effects, card networks have maintained relevance through global interoperability, robust fraud management systems, and adaptability to premium user segments. This analysis underscores a critical insight: the rivalry between UPI and cards is not a zero-sum game but a catalyst for innovation, with each system addressing distinct consumer needs, merchant requirements, and regulatory priorities. The regulatory foresight of the Reserve Bank of India (RBI (Reserve Bank of India)), particularly around data localization and interoperability mandates, has further solidified India’s position as a leader in financial inclusion and digital sovereignty. However, the path forward demands a nuanced understanding of the synergies between these systems rather than a binary choice between them.

For stakeholders, three actionable strategies emerge. First, financial institutions and fintech players must prioritize hybrid solutions that integrate UPI’s immediacy with cards’ global acceptance. For instance, embedding UPI stacks into card-issuing platforms could enable seamless cross-border transactions while retaining cost advantages. Second, merchants should adopt dynamic payment routing tools to optimize transaction success rates and interchange fees, leveraging real-time analytics to route payments through the most efficient channel. Third, policymakers must balance innovation with risk mitigation by accelerating the adoption of AI-driven fraud detection frameworks and standardizing consumer protection protocols across both ecosystems. This includes addressing gaps in liability frameworks for unauthorized UPI transactions, which currently lag behind the chargeback mechanisms entrenched in card networks.

Looking ahead, the convergence of UPI and card technologies could redefine India’s financial landscape. Innovations such as tokenized UPI addresses for international remittances, biometrically secured co-badged cards, and AI-powered credit scoring models using UPI transaction data hold transformative potential. Equally critical is the need to bridge the digital divide: over 190 million Indian adults remain excluded from formal financial systems, despite UPI’s penetration. Expanding no-internet payment modes and enhancing rural digital literacy will be pivotal. Additionally, the RBI’s proposed regulatory sandbox for cross-border payment solutions could position India as a testbed for global payment protocols, leveraging its domestic scale to influence international standards.

Ultimately, the UPI vs. cards debate highlights a broader truth: India’s digital payment success stems from its ability to harmonize competition with collaboration. As the sector evolves, stakeholders must prioritize scalability, security, and inclusivity to sustain momentum. The world is watching how India navigates this delicate balance—its model could inspire emerging economies grappling with similar challenges. To stay at the forefront, action is needed today: fintechs must innovate beyond urban hubs, banks should democratize access to premium payment tools, and regulators must future-proof policies without stifling disruption. The future of finance is not UPI or cards—it’s a synthesis of both, engineered to empower every Indian, from the metro to the hinterland.
